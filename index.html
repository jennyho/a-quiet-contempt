Examining the Stigma of Mental Illness

scroll down

In the context of mental health, stigma is not a physical mark. Instead, it persists as beliefs the public holds against individuals affected by mental illness. Despite the public discourse on mental health and the many initiatives aimed to reduce stigma, it still is a major barrier for those looking for support and treatment.

In the United States...

43.6 million adults had any mental illness in the past year.

9.8 million had a serious mental illness that led to severe functional impairment.

55% of people with any mental illness have not had treatment.

Out of those who do seek treatment, 20% quit prematurely.

Of those dropped out of treatment, 89% also cited social stigma as a reason.

Not only does social stigma disincentivize individuals from seeking help, but it also affects health providers and policymakers, contributing to structural barriers that make getting treatment difficult.

Each year, the General Social Survey (GSS) gets demographic, behavioral, and attitudinal information about the American public. Respondents are randomly selected from the general population. In 2006, the GSS asked numerous questions related to opinions on mental health. These were presented in the form of either statements that respondents agreed or disagreed with or hypothetical situations that involved a mentally ill person.
